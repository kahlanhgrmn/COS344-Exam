Situation-Based Guide

COS344 — Situation-Based Decision Guide

10 Exam Scenarios with Technique Decisions

How to Use This Guide

For each scenario, the exam will describe a scene and ask which CG techniques you'd use. Your answer needs:

  1. The technique name
  2. Why it's appropriate for this scenario
  3. What you'd NOT use and why

Scenario 1: Deep Sea Exploration

Context: Rendering an underwater environment with bioluminescent creatures, volumetric light shafts from the surface, and dense particle effects for water disturbances.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Lighting model | PBR + emissive for bioluminescence | Physically accurate for water, emissive for self-lit creatures |

| Water/transparency | Alpha blending + Fresnel (Schlick) | Semi-transparent water needs blending; Fresnel controls reflection/refraction ratio |

| Refraction | Snell's law (n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂), n_water=1.33 | Light bends entering/exiting water |

| Volumetric effects | Volumetric light shafts (ray marching or texture slices) | Simulates light scattering in water |

| Particles | Particle system with velocity + lifespan | Bubbles, disturbed sediment, floating debris |

| Shadows | No hard shadows — diffuse ambient underwater | Hard shadows don't exist in deep water (diffuse scattering) |

| Optimization | BVH for dense geometry + frustum culling | Many coral/rock meshes; need fast intersection tests |

| Depth effects | Fog with exponential falloff + color shift | Water absorbs red light first → blue-green shift with depth |

| Surface caustics | Projected animated texture map | Refracted light patterns on sea floor |

| Rendering approach | Forward rendering + particle pass | Relatively few lights; particle systems need alpha sorting |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Bioluminescence = emissive material (adds light to scene without a light source)
  • Fresnel equation R(θ,λ) = R₀ + (1-R₀)(1-cosθ)⁵ — grazing angles reflect more
  • Snell's law for refraction direction of light entering water
  • Particle system with per-particle lifespan, velocity, randomness
  • Exponential fog for distance-based water opacity and color absorption
  • BVH for efficient ray/frustum tests against dense coral geometry

What NOT to Use

  • Hard directional shadows — no sun rays penetrate to deep sea
  • Simple ambient lighting alone — misses the dramatic bioluminescent glow
  • BSP trees — designed for indoor static geometry, not open underwater scenes

Scenario 2: Medieval Castle

Context: Rendering a large exterior castle with stone walls, torches, a drawbridge, and characters in armor — broad daylight with hard shadows.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Lighting model | Phong / Blinn-Phong + directional light | Single sun = directional light; Blinn-Phong for stone and metal |

| Shadows | Shadow maps (depth-based) | Hard shadows from directional sun; efficient for large scene |

| Stone texture | Normal maps | Gives stone surface detail without extra geometry |

| Metal armor | PBR specular maps | High-frequency specular highlights on polished metal |

| Distant geometry | LOD (Level of Detail) | Towers far away → simpler mesh; saves vertex processing |

| Culling | Frustum culling + backface culling | Large scene; most walls face away from camera |

| Optimization | BSP tree for interior rooms | If camera enters castle, BSP quickly determines visible rooms |

| Foliage/banners | Alpha discard (discard in shader) | Plant leaves and fabric are not convex — use cutout transparency |

| Ground/terrain | Displacement map or heightmap | Gives bumpy cobblestone terrain with actual geometry |

| Animation | Skeletal animation (FK) for characters | Human characters need joint hierarchy for walking/attacking |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Shadow maps: render scene depth from sun's perspective → compare in main render
  • Normal maps: fake geometric detail on flat stone surfaces per-pixel
  • LOD: swap high-poly tower for low-poly at distance threshold
  • Frustum culling: discard entire towers outside view volume before draw calls
  • BSP tree: binary space partitioning for indoor occlusion determination

What NOT to Use

  • Ray-traced shadows — too expensive for real-time; shadow maps suffice
  • Particle systems — no fire/smoke in this scene description
  • Subsurface scattering — stone and metal don't transmit light

Scenario 3: Martian Colony

Context: Harsh desert landscape on Mars with a colony of metallic habitats, dust storms, and no atmospheric scattering (thin atmosphere).

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Lighting | Strong directional light (no atmosphere scattering) | Mars has thin atmosphere; sunlight is harsh and direct |

| Metal surfaces | PBR with metallic workflow | Physically accurate for polished/brushed metals |

| Terrain | Heightmap + displacement mapping | Rocky Martian terrain needs actual geometry variation |

| Dust storm | Particle system + procedural noise | Millions of dust particles; randomized velocity + direction |

| Shadows | Shadow maps | Harsh directional shadows from sun |

| Sky rendering | Simple color gradient (no scatter) | No atmospheric Rayleigh scattering on Mars |

| Equipment reflection | Environment map (cube map) | Reflects Martian sky and terrain on metallic surfaces |

| Optimization | Frustum culling + LOD | Open landscape; distant habitats can use lower LOD |

| Ground texture | Diffuse + normal map (rust-red) | Unique Martian texture without extra geometry |

| Rendering | Forward rendering | Manageable light count; no complex lighting needed |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • PBR (BRDF) for metal — correct Fresnel, roughness, and metallic reflection
  • Displacement mapping for terrain — modifies actual vertex positions (not just normal)
  • Particle system for dust: N particles with initial velocity, randomized direction, gravity
  • Cube map / environment mapping for metal reflections
  • No atmospheric scattering — don't add blue tint from Rayleigh scattering

What NOT to Use

  • Volumetric fog (atmospheric scattering type) — Mars has near-vacuum atmosphere
  • Subsurface scattering — no organic materials in this scene
  • Soft shadows — thin atmosphere means harder shadows than Earth

Scenario 4: Fantasy Magic Academy

Context: Indoor magic school with glowing spell effects, transparent potion bottles, floating books, and magical energy orbs.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Spell effects | Particle system with emissive particles | Magic = light-emitting particles; use emissive + additive blending |

| Transparency | Alpha blending (back-to-front sorted) | Glass bottles and magical effects need correct transparency |

| Reflective orbs | Environment maps + Fresnel | Crystal orbs reflect surroundings; Fresnel controls edge reflection |

| Area lighting | Point lights + deferred rendering | Many small magical lights need deferred shading |

| Floating objects | Keyframe animation | Floating books = scripted path animation |

| Glow effects | Post-processing bloom | Bright emissive areas bleed light into surrounding pixels |

| Glass/potions | Snell's law refraction + alpha | Correct optical bending through glass |

| Scene structure | Scene graph | Hierarchical transforms for organized indoor scene |

| Shadows | Shadow maps per light | Multiple point lights each cast shadows |

| Optimization | Occlusion culling + BSP | Indoor scene with many rooms; occluded rooms skipped |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Additive blending for magical glow: GL_ONE, GL_ONE — colors only add, never darken
  • Deferred rendering for many point lights — handles N lights efficiently
  • Bloom post-processing — extract bright pixels, blur, composite back
  • Alpha sorting — transparent objects must render back-to-front for correct blending
  • Scene graph — hierarchical scene for indoor room structure

What NOT to Use

  • Backface culling for transparent objects — you need to see through both sides of glass
  • Z-buffer only for transparency — need sorted transparency for correct results
  • Single ambient light — magical scene needs many colored light sources

Scenario 5: Jurassic Safari

Context: Outdoor jungle environment with large dinosaur characters, dense foliage, procedural animation, and dynamic weather.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Dinosaur animation | Skeletal animation (FK + IK) | Complex character rigs; FK for body, IK for foot placement |

| Skin deformation | Smooth skinning d=Σωᵢdᵢ | Blends multiple joint influences for realistic skin |

| Foliage | Alpha discard + LOD | Leaf transparency without sorting issues; LOD for dense trees |

| Dense jungle | Occlusion culling + BVH | Many overlapping objects; need efficient visibility |

| Lighting | Directional (sun) + ambient (sky dome) | Outdoor jungle with light filtering through canopy |

| Rain | Particle system | Falling rain drops with velocity, collision, lifespan |

| Shadows | Shadow maps with PCF (soft) | Natural outdoor shadows are soft, not hard-edged |

| Ground | Displacement mapping | Muddy terrain needs actual geometry bumps |

| Dinosaur scales | Normal maps | High-detail scale texture on character skin |

| Optimization | Frustum culling + LOD | Large outdoor scene; distant trees need simplified meshes |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Smooth skinning formula: d = Σωᵢdᵢ — weights ωᵢ sum to 1, blend joint transforms
  • Inverse kinematics (IK) for foot placement on uneven terrain
  • PCF soft shadows — percentage closer filtering averages multiple shadow map samples
  • Alpha discard for foliage — faster than alpha blending, no sorting required
  • BVH (Bounding Volume Hierarchy) for efficient collision and visibility in dense jungle

What NOT to Use

  • Rigid skinning for dinosaur bodies — causes "candy wrapper" artifacts at joints
  • Hard shadow maps — outdoor natural shadows have penumbra (soft edges)
  • Alpha blending for foliage — sorting thousands of leaves is too expensive

Scenario 6: Steampunk Armada

Context: Fleet of metallic steam-powered airships with brass/copper surfaces, steam exhaust, gears, and dramatic sky battles.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Metal surfaces | PBR (metallic/roughness workflow) | Brass, copper, iron all need physically accurate Fresnel |

| Specular | Environment maps + specular maps | Reflective metal surfaces need sky/scene reflections |

| Fresnel | R(θ,λ) = R₀+(1-R₀)(1-cosθ)⁵ | Controls edge vs normal-incidence reflectance |

| Steam exhaust | Particle system + volumetric rendering | Steam = many small semi-transparent particles |

| Gears (animation) | Keyframe or procedural rotation | Mechanical rotation = simple timed rotation matrix |

| Lighting | Directional sun + sky ambient | Outdoor aerial scene |

| Shadows | Shadow maps | Ships casting shadows on each other |

| Optimization | Frustum culling + LOD | Distant ships in fleet use lower detail |

| Scene | Scene graph with instances | Many similar ships = instanced rendering |

| Rendering | Forward + deferred hybrid | Manageable lights but PBR needs proper shading pass |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • PBR metallic workflow: albedo + metallic + roughness maps per surface
  • R₀(λ) = ((n(λ)-1)/(n(λ)+1))² — base reflectance at normal incidence for each metal
  • Environment mapping (cubemap) — sky reflection in polished metal
  • Instanced rendering — draw many similar ships with one draw call
  • Particle system for steam with lifespan, velocity, opacity falloff

What NOT to Use

  • Phong alone — Phong doesn't capture the metallic/dielectric distinction properly
  • Flat normals on gear teeth — need normal maps for mechanical detail
  • Subsurface scattering — metal is opaque

Scenario 7: Microscopic Cell World

Context: Visualizing the interior of a biological cell — organelles, membranes, DNA, and ATP molecules at molecular scale.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Translucency | Alpha blending (additive/subtractive) | Cell membranes are semi-transparent; need to see organelles inside |

| Organic glow | Emissive + bloom | Some organelles emit fluorescent light |

| Subsurface scattering | SSS approximation | Organic material like membranes transmit scattered light |

| Shadows | Diffuse ambient only (no hard shadows) | Biological scale; light is diffusely scattered everywhere |

| Surface detail | Normal maps | Bumpy membrane texture without excess geometry |

| Animation | Physics-based / procedural | Brownian motion of molecules = random walk procedural |

| Particles | Particle system for ATP, vesicles | Floating molecular components with random motion |

| Scale | No parallax/perspective distortion | Microscopic — orthographic projection may be more appropriate |

| Rendering | Forward + alpha sorted | Transparency is key; need correct sorting |

| Optimization | BVH | Dense packing of organelles needs spatial indexing |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Additive blending for glowing cytoplasm: GL_SRC_ALPHA, GL_ONE
  • Alpha compositing c = αc_f + (1-α)c_b for layered membrane transparency
  • Orthographic projection — microscopic view doesn't have strong perspective
  • Procedural animation for Brownian motion (random displacement each frame)
  • SSS — membranes are thin biological materials that transmit scattered light

What NOT to Use

  • Hard directional light — light at cellular scale is diffuse omnidirectional
  • LOD — objects are already at minimal detail; no benefit
  • Backface culling — need to see through membranes from both sides

Scenario 8: Arctic Research Station

Context: Snow and ice landscape with metallic research buildings, frozen water surfaces, blizzard conditions, and aurora in the sky.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Ice/snow | PBR + high roughness for snow, low for ice | Snow = rough diffuse; ice = smooth specular with Fresnel |

| Ice reflections | Environment map + Fresnel | Smooth ice reflects sky; Fresnel increases at grazing angles |

| Blizzard | Particle system + wind velocity field | Snow particles with velocity, lifetime, turbulence |

| Aurora | Emissive screen-space effect / additive | Aurora = color-shifting emissive light in sky |

| Metal buildings | PBR metallic workflow | Cold metal surfaces with correct specular |

| Shadows | Shadow maps (directional sun or moon) | Hard shadows from cold light source |

| Ground | Displacement map for snow drift | Actual geometry variation for snow mounds |

| Optimization | Frustum culling + LOD | Open landscape; distant features need LOD |

| Depth of field | Post-processing DoF | Blizzard visibility limits — simulate with blur |

| Lighting | Directional + sky hemisphere | Low-angle sun, bounced light from snow |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Fresnel — ice acts like a dielectric: R₀ for ice ≈ 0.02-0.05, high reflection at grazing
  • Particle system for blizzard: spawn rate, velocity with wind field, fade out at ground
  • Screen-space fog/DoF to simulate low visibility in snow storm
  • Snow accumulation — displacement map updated over time
  • Hemisphere lighting — snow-covered ground bounces light upward (two-sided ambient)

What NOT to Use

  • Subsurface scattering — snow does scatter light but computationally expensive for environment
  • Bioluminescence / emissive for snow — snow reflects, doesn't emit
  • Deferred rendering — limited light sources in arctic scene; forward is simpler

Scenario 9: Post-Apocalyptic City

Context: Ruined urban environment with damaged buildings, rusted vehicles, fire and smoke, and dynamic weather — emphasis on gritty realism.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Metal rust/damage | PBR with roughness/metallic maps | Varying rust/clean areas need per-pixel material variation |

| Structural detail | Normal + displacement maps | Cracked concrete, bent metal without extreme polygon count |

| Fire & smoke | Particle system | Fire = emissive particles; smoke = alpha-blended billboard particles |

| Urban occlusion | BSP tree / PVS | Dense buildings occlude each other; precomputed visibility |

| Shadows | Shadow maps + PCF | Dramatic directional light (post-storm sky) |

| Rain | Particle system | Falling rain with splash particles on impact |

| Lighting | Multiple point lights (fires) + deferred | Many fire light sources → deferred shading |

| LOD | LOD for distant buildings | Many buildings; far ones need simplified meshes |

| Glass shards | Alpha blending + physics | Broken window glass needs transparency and debris physics |

| Rendering | Deferred shading | Many dynamic point lights from fires |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Deferred shading — G-buffer stores position/normal/albedo; lighting pass handles N lights
  • BSP/PVS — urban scene where one building fully occludesanother
  • Particle system for fire: emissive, upward velocity, additive blending, short lifespan
  • PBR material maps — roughness varies from clean metal (low) to rust (high)
  • Depth prepass — render scene depth first to avoid expensive pixel shader on occluded geometry

What NOT to Use

  • Forward rendering with many lights — too many fire lights for forward to handle efficiently
  • Single ambient only — misses dramatic directional light and fire glow
  • No occlusion culling — city geometry is extremely dense; culling is essential

Scenario 10: Cyberpunk City

Context: Night-time futuristic city with neon lights, rain-wet reflective streets, holographic displays, and massive crowds.

Decision Table

| Requirement | Chosen Technique | Why |

|-------------|-----------------|-----|

| Many light sources | Deferred rendering + many point lights | Hundreds of neon signs = many point lights → deferred shading essential |

| Neon glow | Emissive + bloom post-processing | Neon = emissive surfaces; bloom spreads light into surrounding pixels |

| Wet streets | PBR + environment map + low roughness | Wet surface = smooth/mirror-like; Fresnel increases at grazing |

| Rain puddles | Screen-space reflections + ripple animation | Dynamic water surface reflecting neon lights |

| Holograms | Additive alpha blending | Holographic displays are semi-transparent, add color on top |

| Crowds | Skeletal animation + LOD (impostors at distance) | Many characters; distant ones = flat textured billboards |

| City occlusion | PVS + BSP | Pre-computed visibility for dense urban grid |

| Depth prepass | Depth prepass before main shading | Many overlapping surfaces; avoid expensive deferred pass on hidden geometry |

| LOD | LOD for buildings + crowds | Massive scene; far geometry greatly simplified |

| Rendering | Full deferred pipeline | Only option for hundreds of dynamic lights |

Key Techniques to Mention

  • Deferred rendering with G-buffer — mandatory for this many lights
  • Bloom post-processing: bright-pass extract → Gaussian blur → additive composite
  • PVS (Potentially Visible Set) — precomputed which city blocks see which others
  • Instanced rendering for crowd — thousands of similar character meshes
  • Screen-space reflections (SSR) for wet street reflections — efficient, uses depth buffer
  • Impostors / billboards for very distant crowd members — 2D textured quads

What NOT to Use

  • Forward rendering — completely impractical for hundreds of lights
  • Hard shadow maps for every neon light — too many shadow maps; use shadow map atlases or skip for distant lights
  • High-poly meshes for distant crowd — use impostors/billboards instead

| Technique | Scenarios | When to Use |

|-----------|-----------|------------|

| Instanced rendering | 6,10 | Many identical or similar objects |

Exam Strategy

  1. Read the scenario — identify: outdoor/indoor, material types, dynamic elements, scale
  2. Lighting first — what type of light (directional/point/emissive)? How many lights? → Forward vs Deferred
  3. Materials — metals? Glass? Organic? → PBR vs Phong; Fresnel?
  4. Transparency — any? → Alpha blending (sorted) or discard
  5. Animation — characters? Particles? Mechanical? → Skeletal/particle/keyframe
  6. Optimization — dense scene? Many objects? → Frustum/occlusion culling, LOD, BSP/PVS/BVH
  7. Special effects — glow? Fog? Rain? → Post-processing, particles, volumetric

Always justify why — "I use shadow maps because there is a clear directional light source" scores more than just naming the technique.
